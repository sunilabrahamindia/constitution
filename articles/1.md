**Article 1** defines the constitutional identity of India, establishing the country as *India, that is Bharat*, and describing it as a **Union of States**. It outlines the components of India's territory and anchors the structure for how States and Union Territories are organised within the Constitution.

<div class="full-text-box" id="fulltext" markdown="1">

## Full Text (Authoritative)

**1. (1)** *India, that is Bharat, shall be a Union of States.*

**(2)** *The States and the territories thereof shall be as specified in the First Schedule.*

**(3)** *The territory of India shall comprise ‚Äî*  
**(a)** *the territories of the States;*  
**(b)** *the Union territories specified in the First Schedule; and*  
**(c)** *such other territories as may be acquired.*

</div>

<div class="summary-box" markdown="1">

## Summary

- India has two constitutionally recognised names: **India** and **Bharat**.  
- The framers used the term **Union of States** to emphasise unity and non-secession.  
- The First Schedule lists the **States and Union Territories**.  
- India‚Äôs territory includes any area that may be lawfully acquired in the future.

</div>

<div class="explanation-box" markdown="1">

## Explanation

Article 1 introduces the territorial and political identity of the Republic.  
The expression **Union of States** was carefully chosen to reflect:

- the permanence of the Union,  
- the absence of a right to secession, and  
- the historical context of integrating princely States.

Clause (3) provides a flexible structure, enabling the Union to incorporate new territory, reorganise internal boundaries, and update the First Schedule without altering the core definition of India.

</div>


<div class="history-box" markdown="1">

## Historical Background

During the Constituent Assembly debates:

- Some members preferred **Bharat** as the sole name; others insisted on **India** for international continuity.  
- Dr B. R. Ambedkar explained that the term *Union* signified an indestructible arrangement, unlike a federation created by treaty.  
- With over 500 princely States being integrated, flexibility was essential for future reorganisation.

</div>


<div class="judgment-box" markdown="1">

## Key Judicial Interpretation

### **Berubari Union Case (1960)**  
The Supreme Court held that **cession of territory** requires a constitutional amendment under Article 368.

### **State Reorganisation Judgments**  
Courts have repeatedly affirmed that Parliament‚Äôs power under Articles 2‚Äì4:

- is **broad**,  
- does **not require mandatory State consent**,  
- and is central to the stability of the Union.

</div>
